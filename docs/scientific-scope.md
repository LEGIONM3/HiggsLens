# HiggsLens Scientific Scope & Objectives

## 1. What HiggsLens Studies
HiggsLens is an educational and machine-learning research platform designed to explore simulated collision data from the ATLAS experiment at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). Specifically, it investigates Higgs boson production decaying into two tau leptons ($H \to \tau\tau$).

The application examines how well tabular machine learning algorithms can separate rare signal topologies ($H \to \tau\tau$) from dominant background processes ($Z \to \tau\tau$, top-quark pair production $t\bar{t}$, and $W+\text{jets}$).

## 2. What HiggsLens Cannot Conclude
- **No Real Collider Discovery**: All events analyzed are Monte Carlo simulations with a fixed Higgs mass ($125\text{ GeV}$). A high model prediction score indicates similarity to signal simulation templates, **not** the discovery of a physical Higgs boson in experimental data.
- **No Raw Reconstruction**: Tabular input variables (`PRI_*`) represent already-reconstructed physical objects (hadronic taus, leptons, jets, missing energy) generated by the ATLAS detector simulation and physics algorithms. HiggsLens does not process raw electronic detector signals or track reconstruction.

## 3. Disambiguation of Terms
- **Simulated Event**: A single Monte Carlo collision event with exact kinematics and true process labels ($s$ or $b$).
- **Visualization Payload**: A geometric representation of measured primary detector objects (`tau`, `lepton`, `jets`, `MET`) for frontend display. It does not display unmeasured tracks or unverified physics particles.
- **Model Prediction**: A calibrated statistical probability \(P(s \mid \mathbf{x})\) that an event vector $\mathbf{x}$ matches the signal distribution.
- **Scientific Discovery**: Requires 5-sigma statistical significance observed across real detector data after systematic uncertainties and background modeling checks—far outside the scope of binary classification on a fixed benchmark dataset.
